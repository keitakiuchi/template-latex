# Professional Editing Feedback Checklist

Apply this checklist under the conditions defined in the `Checklist Policy` of `../SKILL.md`. When applicable, read it before writing or integration, use it throughout the work, and complete the Final Pass before returning the output. It is derived from professional manuscript-editing feedback and should be applied without changing the underlying scientific meaning.

## Tone and Word Choice

- Replace casual verbs and phrases with formal academic alternatives.
- Use field-appropriate terminology, especially in medical and scientific writing.
- Use "personal information" for a general ethics/privacy discussion when that is the field's intended construct. Preserve legally or technically defined alternatives such as "personal data," "protected health information," or an official variable label when they apply.
- Use "80% power" as standard statistical terminology.
- Use "study" for the research work conducted and "article," "paper," or "manuscript" for the written report. Do not use "paper" when referring to experiments, analyses, or procedures carried out as part of the study.
- Prefer precise academic collocations for joint agency, such as "jointly produce," when several sources, agents, or raters create an observed output.
- Avoid contractions; use full forms such as "do not," "cannot," and "would not."
- Check collocations and idiomatic prepositions. Prefer standard academic pairings such as "arrive at a conclusion," "associated with," "related to," "consistent with," and "outside the scope of."
- Prefer a concrete grammatical subject and direct verb when an abstract or metonymic subject obscures what actually changed, measured, or acted. For example, distinguish changing a procedure from changing the ratings produced by that procedure.
- Remove empty frames such as "there are several" or "it is necessary to" when a direct subject and verb state the same meaning more clearly. Retain the frame when existence, discourse focus, or impersonal obligation is genuinely the point.
- Use transitions to express a specific relation—addition, contrast, cause, consequence, or qualification. Do not add or vary transitions merely for surface variety.

## Tense, Modality, and Claim Boundaries

- Use past tense for completed study procedures and observed results. Use present tense for general principles, established knowledge, and statements about what an article, table, or figure shows. Use future or conditional forms for work not yet conducted.
- Preserve a purposeful tense contrast within a paragraph. For example, do not change the general principle "improved fluency does not entail therapeutic improvement" into the empirical claim "improved fluency did not lead to improvement" unless the study tested that causal relationship.
- Preserve modal verbs, negation, and hedging. Treat changes from "may," "could," or "would" to an unqualified assertion as substantive edits, not routine polishing.
- Distinguish obligation, recommendation, possibility, and capability. Use "must" for a supported requirement, "should" for advice or a normative recommendation, and "may/could" or "can" only for the intended possibility or capability. Clarify vague "need to" wording when its force matters, but do not replace it mechanically with a stronger modal.
- Preserve the object and boundary of a claim. "Additional evidence would be needed for broader claims" is not equivalent to "additional studies are required to validate these claims."
- Keep indirect indicators indirect. Do not rewrite flags, screening rules, potential risks, or proxy measures as confirmed events or validated outcomes.

## Articles, Number, and Construct Scope

- Use the definite article for a specific previously identified entity or measure; usually omit it for generic plural or mass-noun statements. Do not insert articles mechanically.
- Preserve number when it identifies the study unit. Distinguish one evaluated system from systems in general, one response-rendering layer from multiple layers, and one aggregate construct from individual observations.
- Avoid a possessive that falsely narrows an aggregate measure. When ratings summarize many simulated clients or scripts, "client naturalness" may be accurate whereas "the client's naturalness" may imply one client.
- Preserve official capitalization and number in construct names, scale labels, variable names, and component labels. A fluent pluralization or lowercasing can still change the defined measure.
- Check whether a noun is being used as a mass concept or as countable instances before pluralizing terms such as "variation," "agreement," "behavior change," or "evidence."
- Give pronouns and demonstratives a clear antecedent. Replace a bare "it," "this," "these," or "those" with a specific noun when more than one plausible referent is available, but retain concise reference when the antecedent is unmistakable.

## Concision

- Replace wordy phrases with concise alternatives.
- Prefer direct verbs or gerunds over noun forms, such as "evaluated" rather than "performed an evaluation of," "for generating" rather than "for generation of," and "rather than relying on" rather than "rather than a reliance on."
- When alternatives are coordinated, favor parallel grammatical forms. Where the construction permits, replace an abstract noun phrase with a direct verb or gerund, such as "rely on" or "relying on" rather than "place reliance on" or "a reliance on."
- Remove repeated head nouns in coordinated series when a single shared head or label remains unambiguous, such as "first-, fifth-, and sixth-grade students" and "Layers 3 and 4" rather than "Layers 3 and Layers 4."
- Remove repeated articles or determiners in a coordinated series when one can clearly govern all items, such as "either the retained drafts or final responses" rather than "either the retained drafts or the final responses." Retain the second determiner if it marks a genuinely separate referent, contrast, or scope.
- Reduce repeated heads in coordinated noun phrases, such as "agent-based and human evaluations" rather than "agent-based evaluations and human evaluations."
- Share repeated modifiers across coordinated items when their scope over every item is clear, such as "newly generated and rated scripts" rather than "newly generated and newly rated scripts." Repeat the modifier when sharing it could change or obscure its scope.
- Avoid redundant articles at the beginning of titles and headings.

## Grammar and Structure

- Hyphenate compound adjectives before nouns when the words jointly modify the noun: "long-term effects," "low-impact exercise program," "middle-aged adults."
- Use hyphenation selectively for compound modifiers, not mechanically for every adjacent noun. For example, "stress-test functions" is clearer when both words jointly modify "functions."
- Recast ambiguous front-loaded noun stacks when hyphenation alone does not clarify the relationship. For example, "AI counseling simulations with synthetic clients" can be clearer than "synthetic-client AI counseling simulations" because it shows that the clients are synthetic and the counseling is simulated.
- Reposition misplaced modifiers close to the word or phrase they modify.
- Place adverbs where they clearly modify the intended verb or phrase, such as "randomized independently for each rater" and "used only as aids."
- Balance comparative and alternative structures by matching their grammatical frames and comparing like with like.
- Repeat the relevant preposition when paired alternatives require it for balance or clarity, such as "evaluated through simulated interactions rather than through direct deployment."
- Use "that" or "those," usually with the appropriate preposition, when the omitted comparison head would otherwise cause a property, measure, or rating to be compared directly with an entity, such as "broader than that of the other ratings" or "higher than that for sample B."
- Use a direct negative contrast such as "does not reflect X but rather Y" instead of placing "not" after the verb in a way that makes the contrast difficult to parse.
- Keep paired negative complements parallel, such as "interpreted as X and not as Y" or "belongs to stage X and not to stage Y."
- Use a comma before a coordinating conjunction when it joins two independent clauses. Use a semicolon, period, or conjunction rather than a comma alone between independent clauses.
- Retain a repeated preposition, article, or subordinator when it is needed to mark scope. Concision does not justify deleting a second "because," "for," or "as" if the remaining phrase becomes ambiguous.
- Keep every coordinated list parallel in grammatical form and semantic role. Confirm that one introductory verb, preposition, article, or modifier can govern every item before sharing it across the series.
- Use the serial comma consistently when required by the journal or selected style.
- Use "respectively" only for parallel lists with a clear one-to-one mapping.
- In American English, use "that" for essential clauses and "which" for nonessential clauses.

## Spelling and Hyphenation Consistency

- Hyphenate a multiword expression used before a noun when its words jointly form a compound modifier and the selected journal or house style calls for hyphenation, such as "post-hoc LLM scores" under a style that hyphenates "post-hoc" attributively.
- Do not apply hyphenation mechanically. Check the journal style, an authoritative dictionary, or the manuscript's selected convention, especially for established open, closed, and hyphenated forms.
- Search the full manuscript for competing forms and select one consistently, such as "nonrepetitive" versus "non-repetitive." Apply the same form in the abstract, main text, tables, figures, and supplementary material unless a quoted term or official name must be preserved.
- Treat consistency as a manuscript-wide check: a form can be locally grammatical yet still be incorrect for the document if another spelling or hyphenation convention has already been selected.
- Do not infer a universal rule for prefixes such as "pre-," "post-," "non-," "inter-," "re-," or "over-" from one editor's choice. Check the journal style, dictionary, and existing manuscript convention for each term.
- Do not hyphenate a compound merely because it precedes a noun when the first element is an `-ly` adverb or the expression is an established open form. Check both grammatical position and lexical convention.
- Preserve established multiword constructs as open nouns, including "change talk" and "sustain talk." Add attributive hyphenation only when the field and journal permit it and the official term remains recognizable.
- Exempt official framework names, scale names, quoted wording, and reference titles from global spelling normalization. For example, do not change an official use of "Health Care" to "Healthcare" merely to match surrounding prose.
- Keep statistical labels and symbols consistent. If the selected style uses forms such as "M =" and "SD =," apply them coherently rather than inserting an equals sign for only one statistic.
- Apply the selected typography consistently, including straight versus typographic quotation marks and apostrophes, hyphens versus en dashes, and spacing around symbols. Treat these as style choices unless the punctuation carries mathematical or semantic meaning.
- Use ordinary lowercase for generic statistical indices, constructs, and common nouns unless they begin a sentence or belong to an official name. Do not preserve unnecessary capitalization as a stylistic preference.

## Abbreviations, Tables, and Figures

- Define each abbreviation at first use in the abstract.
- Define each abbreviation again at first use in the main text.
- Define abbreviations used in tables within the table legend or footnotes.
- Define abbreviations used in figures within the figure legend.
- Keep abbreviations consistent across text, tables, figures, and supplementary material.
- After defining an abbreviation within a document component, normally use the abbreviation consistently rather than repeatedly alternating with the full form. Re-expand only when the venue, a long separation, or a stand-alone section requires it.
- Match named sections and cross-references to the source exactly. For example, refer to the "Methods section" when the heading is "Methods," and preserve the selected names and numbering of tables, figures, appendices, and supplements.
- Search the final manuscript for untranslated or non-target-language remnants, placeholder text, field codes, and malformed citation commands that may survive conversion or revision.

## Numeric and Methodological Noun Audit

- Check not only each number but also the noun, denominator, time point, group, unit, and independence claim attached to it. The same numeral can become scientifically wrong when "observations" is changed to "participants," "ratings" to "scripts," or repeated measures to "independent" cases.
- Preserve the level of analysis across prose, equations, tables, and figures. Distinguish participants, sessions, trajectories, scripts, ratings, raters, items, and model-level observations whenever the design does.
- Verify that model descriptions match the analysis: outcome scale, link function, fixed and random effects, reference category, estimand, and interpretation. Treat a fluent change from ordinal to continuous, or from association to effect, as a substantive error.
- Confirm that the grammatical subject of a Methods or Results sentence is the entity that was generated, rated, modified, fitted, or analyzed. Do not let a procedure stand in for its outputs when that changes the claim.
- Preserve statistical direction and uncertainty together with the estimate. Do not detach a confidence interval, threshold, comparison group, or "respectively" mapping from the value it qualifies.

## Applying Proofed Files to Source Manuscripts

- Treat a proofed DOCX, PDF markup, or edited copy as a candidate revision. Check meaning before accepting each substantive edit.
- Identify the file that the editor actually received. When the current manuscript contains later author revisions, compare the pre-proof source with the proofed file first, then merge the verified editorial changes into the current manuscript.
- Inspect tracked changes and comments before extracting text. If the proofed DOCX is a clean final copy, use the unproofed DOCX, version history, or another contemporaneous source as the comparison base.
- When learning from several proofed files, group successive versions of the same underlying manuscript into one manuscript family before judging recurrence. Repeated edits across versions are not independent evidence.
- Use the editor's original-language comment as the authority. Treat machine-generated comment translations as aids, not as additional evidence or a source of wording rules.
- Treat generic comments about flow, clarity, or native tone as low-information labels. Infer any reusable lesson from the actual original-to-revised contrast and surrounding context.
- Separate general English rules from journal formatting, author preference, domain terminology, and document-specific content queries. Verify mutable journal requirements from current official instructions rather than encoding an old editor comment as a permanent rule.
- Preserve the source manuscript's authoritative structure unless the user requests structural conversion: headings, table panels, table notes, figure captions, local image paths, raw Markdown/OpenXML blocks, supplement references, and reference lists.
- Compare the source and proofed versions at multiple levels: title, abstract, section headings, paragraph sequence, tables, figures, notes, references, citations, URLs/DOIs, and appendix or supplement pointers.
- Watch for fluent but meaning-changing edits to study constructs, scale names, intervention or component labels, population descriptors, sampling frame, study design, estimands, outcomes, statistical direction, and claim boundaries.
- Keep established domain terms even when a proofed alternative looks natural in general English. For example, preserve "sustain talk" in motivational interviewing and avoid changing population descriptors such as "middle-aged single men" into wording that changes marital, household, or demographic meaning.
- Preserve statistics and technical notation exactly unless the source is clearly erroneous: sample sizes, percentages, odds ratios, confidence intervals, p values, rating ranges, thresholds, and table values.
- Treat line wrapping, smart quotes, escaped brackets, generated `media/` image paths, table reflow, and Unicode punctuation as conversion artifacts unless the document comparison shows that the editor intentionally changed them.
- If a proofed file ends before acknowledgments, disclosures, appendices, or references, determine whether proofreading was limited to the main text. Do not interpret an out-of-scope omission as an instruction to delete source content.
- If a proofed conversion drops content such as reference entries, table panels, image links, or reviewer-facing masked links, restore that content from the source manuscript.
- Preserve post-proof author additions and structural revisions unless a verified editorial change applies directly to the same passage and can be merged without changing meaning.
- Maintain an accept/reject/verify disposition for substantive edits. "Verify" is required when a revision changes modality, the acting subject, a construct, a number's unit, model interpretation, comparison group, or claim boundary.
- After integration, run or perform checks appropriate to the format, such as Markdown/Word conversion, diff review, heading counts, reference counts, DOI/URL comparison, numeric-token comparison, and searches for known risky substitutions.

## Self-Proofreading Protocol

Use separate passes so that improving fluency does not conceal meaning drift:

1. **Lock meaning:** record the study design, units of analysis, primary claims, direction of key results, modal boundaries, validated terms, and immutable numbers before editing.
2. **Edit for native academic prose:** work paragraph by paragraph for purpose, information order, direct verbs, clear subjects, natural collocations, modifier placement, parallel structure, and concision.
3. **Audit the edits contrastively:** compare each revised sentence with its source. Mark any change to modality, negation, tense function, subject, object, number, unit, comparison group, causal force, or citation scope for explicit verification.
4. **Run manuscript-wide consistency searches:** abbreviations and their first uses; competing spellings and hyphenation; capitalization; labels; quotation marks and dashes; headings; cross-references; tables, figures, supplements, citations, and disclosures.
5. **Read the clean revision as a new document:** check paragraph progression, antecedents, transition logic, sentence rhythm, and whether titles, abstract, sections, tables, and figures still describe the same study.
6. **Complete the Final Pass below:** resolve every verify item or report it rather than silently guessing.

## Example Transformations

| Issue | Less suitable | Better academic English |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Casual medical verb | The dose was given daily. | The dose was administered daily. |
| Wordy phrase | all over the world | globally; worldwide |
| Compound adjective | The study examined the long term effects of a low impact exercise program on the cardiovascular health of middle aged adults. | The study examined the long-term effects of a low-impact exercise program on the cardiovascular health of middle-aged adults. |
| Collocation | arrived on the same conclusion | arrived at the same conclusion |
| Formal joint agency | The system and raters co-produced the transcript. | The system and raters jointly produced the transcript. |
| Paper vs. study | This paper did not evaluate whether the intervention improved outcomes. | This study did not evaluate whether the intervention improved outcomes. |
| Misplaced modifier | The results correlated with predictions by the model significantly. | The results significantly correlated with model predictions. |
| Adverb placement | These outputs were used as aids only. | These outputs were used only as aids. |
| Repeated preposition in alternatives | The systems were evaluated through simulations rather than direct deployment. | The systems were evaluated through simulations rather than through direct deployment. |
| Negative contrast | The result reflects not poor readability, but an inconclusive contrast. | The result does not reflect poor readability but rather an inconclusive contrast. |
| Parallel negative complement | The findings belong to the audit stage, not to clinical readiness. | The findings belong to the audit stage and not to clinical readiness. |
| Comma splice | Rating was performed as coauthor work, no compensation was paid. | Rating was performed as coauthor work; no compensation was paid. |
| Unbalanced comparison | The variation intensity for sample A is higher than sample B. | The variation intensity for sample A is higher than that for sample B. |
| Comparison with an omitted head | The overall rating was broader than the others. | The overall rating was broader than that of the others. |
| Repeated noun in a series | The teacher taught first-grade students, fifth-grade students, and sixth-grade students. | The teacher taught first-, fifth-, and sixth-grade students. |
| Repeated label in a series | The contrast was between Layers 3 and Layers 4. | The contrast was between Layers 3 and 4. |
| Repeated article in alternatives | We presented either the retained drafts or the final responses. | We presented either the retained drafts or final responses. |
| Repeated head noun | Agent-based evaluations and human evaluations provide complementary evidence. | Agent-based and human evaluations provide complementary evidence. |
| Ambiguous noun stack | Synthetic-client AI counseling simulations were evaluated. | AI counseling simulations with synthetic clients were evaluated. |
| Ethics/privacy terminology | private information; individual information | personal information |
| Statistical terminology | 80% statistical power | 80% power |
| Nominalization | We performed an evaluation of the methods. | We evaluated the methods. |
| Noun form after preposition | The API was used for generation of the scripts. | The API was used for generating the scripts. |
| Nominalized alternative | We should collect new data rather than place reliance on translated text. | We should collect new data rather than rely on translated text. |
| Repeated shared modifier | We used newly generated and newly rated scripts. | We used newly generated and rated scripts. |
| Inconsistent spelling | The text uses both nonrepetitive and non-repetitive. | Select the journal-preferred form and use it throughout. |

## Risky Proof Edits to Reject or Repair

| Risk | Source meaning | Risky proofed wording | Why it is risky |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Lost modality | Edits may alter therapeutic intent. | Edits alter therapeutic intent. | Converts a possibility into a confirmed effect. |
| Changed claim object | More evidence would be needed for broader claims. | More studies are required to validate these claims. | Recasts a boundary on future claims as doubt about the current findings. |
| Logical claim changed to empirical result | Improved fluency does not entail therapeutic improvement. | Improved fluency did not lead to therapeutic improvement. | Changes a general logical distinction into an untested causal result. |
| Aggregate measure narrowed | Client naturalness was moderate across the scripts. | The client's naturalness was moderate. | Implies one client rather than an aggregate construct. |
| Construct renamed | Raters identified sustain talk. | Raters identified sustaining talk. | Replaces an established motivational interviewing term. |
| Proxy promoted to outcome | The audit flagged potential intent changes. | The audit identified intent changes. | Converts an indirect flag into a confirmed finding. |
| Unit of analysis changed | The analysis included 120 rater-level observations. | The analysis included 120 independent scripts. | Attaches the same number to a different unit and adds an unsupported independence claim. |
| Acting subject changed | Re-rating changed several ratings. | The re-rating procedure was changed. | Changes the outputs that were modified into a claim that the procedure itself changed. |
| Model scale changed | The ordinal model treated the ratings as ordered categories. | The ordinal model treated the ratings as continuous. | Contradicts the stated outcome scale and changes the model interpretation. |
| Obligation strengthened | Authors may consider an additional analysis. | Authors must conduct an additional analysis. | Converts an option into a requirement without an authoritative basis. |

## Final Pass

Before returning drafted or edited text, or a proofed-file integration, check:

- Scientific meaning is unchanged.
- No result is overstated beyond the provided data.
- Abbreviations are defined in each required location.
- Titles and headings are concise.
- Tables and figures can stand alone.
- References, citations, URLs/DOIs, tables, figures, and supplementary references have not been lost during format conversion.
- Study-specific terminology, construct names, population descriptors, and claim boundaries still match the source manuscript.
- Tense matches the rhetorical function of each statement, and modal verbs, negation, and hedging retain their original scope.
- Articles, singular/plural forms, and possessives preserve the intended referent and unit of analysis.
- Every number still modifies the correct unit, group, denominator, time point, and independence status.
- Methods and Results use the correct acting subject and describe the model, outcome scale, and estimand consistently.
- Sentences are readable without unnecessary repetition.
- Comparative and alternative structures are grammatically balanced and compare like with like.
- Independent clauses are joined with appropriate punctuation rather than a comma splice.
- Variant spellings and hyphenation are consistent across the full manuscript.
- Abbreviations are used consistently after definition, and no placeholders, field codes, malformed citation commands, or non-target-language remnants remain.
- Every substantive edit marked "verify" has been resolved or reported to the user.
- Format-conversion artifacts have not been mistaken for editorial changes, and post-proof author revisions remain intact.
