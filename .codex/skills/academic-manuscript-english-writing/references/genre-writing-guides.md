## Shared Intake and Authority

Before drafting or substantially revising a document:

1. Identify the genre, target journal or venue, audience, word or character limit, required structure, and selected English variety.
2. Identify the authoritative source for facts, statistics, terminology, citations, and manuscript structure.
3. Distinguish language work from substantive research work. Do not invent missing evidence, analyses, policies, editor names, reviewer positions, or submission declarations.
4. Preserve placeholders when required information is unavailable, or mark the missing input explicitly outside the proposed prose.
5. Follow this priority order: scientific meaning and supplied facts; explicit venue instructions; disciplinary conventions; consistency within the current manuscript; general defaults in this guide.

## Titles and Headings

- State the central topic, population, exposure or intervention, outcome, or study design only when the manuscript supports it.
- Prefer searchable, specific terms over promotional language.
- Keep the grammatical relationship between concepts unambiguous; recast dense noun stacks when their scope is unclear.
- Avoid claiming causality, effectiveness, validation, novelty, or generalizability beyond the study design and evidence.
- Omit an initial article when it adds no meaning and the journal style permits omission.
- Preserve official construct names, intervention names, acronyms, and study labels.
- Keep headings parallel at the same hierarchy level and ensure that each heading accurately describes its section.
- When a title changes, synchronize the full title across the manuscript, title page, submission metadata, cover letter, and other supplied submission files. Keep a separately required running title concise and consistent in meaning rather than forcing it to be identical.

## Manuscript Sections

- Draft from the supplied outline, evidence map, notes, results, or source text. Do not fill evidentiary gaps with plausible content.
- Give each paragraph one controlling purpose. Connect evidence to interpretation without repeating the same claim in several forms.
- Use past tense for completed methods and observed results; use present tense for established knowledge, the argument advanced by the manuscript, and what tables or figures show.
- Separate results from interpretation when the target genre or journal expects that distinction.
- Preserve calibrated claim strength. Match causal language to causal evidence and associative language to observational evidence.
- Use transitions to clarify logical relationships, not to decorate every paragraph.
- Maintain section-specific restraint: procedural precision in Methods, factual reporting in Results, and bounded interpretation in Discussion.
- Preserve citation locations unless moving one is necessary for unambiguous attribution; never imply that a citation supports a different claim merely by repositioning it.
- Flag attribution phrases such as "previous research," "it has been reported," or "evidence suggests" when the supplied text does not identify supporting citations. Do not invent the missing source.
- Choose active or passive voice by informational focus. Use an explicit author subject for study actions when the venue permits it and doing so removes ambiguity; use passive voice when the procedure or object appropriately carries the focus.

## Abstracts

- Make the abstract understandable without relying on the main text while staying within the venue's required format and limit.
- For an empirical study, cover the research context or gap, objective, methods, principal results, and conclusion. Use labeled headings only when required or clearly appropriate.
- Report the study design, population, setting, sample size, primary measure, or analytical approach when each is material and supported by the manuscript.
- Include concrete principal results rather than vague statements such as “significant improvement” when the source provides the relevant estimate and uncertainty.
- Keep every number, direction, group label, time point, and statistical qualifier consistent with the main text and tables.
- Do not introduce a claim, citation, abbreviation, outcome, or recommendation that does not appear in the manuscript or supplied source material.
- Define necessary abbreviations independently in the abstract. Remove nonessential abbreviations that save little space.
- Match the conclusion to the reported results and study design; do not use the abstract to strengthen the paper's claims.
- Supply only the requested language. Do not generate a second-language abstract unless the user or venue requests one.

## Tables and Figures

### Table titles and notes

- Write a concise title that identifies what is summarized without repeating every column heading.
- Make the table interpretable without requiring the reader to search the main text for groups, measures, time points, or units.
- Define abbreviations, symbols, model labels, and nonstandard statistical notation in notes when the venue requires them.
- Distinguish general notes, specific notes, and significance notes according to the selected style.
- Preserve exact row and column labels, values, units, and reference categories. Do not rename a variable merely to improve fluency.
- Avoid interpreting results in a title or note unless the journal expects an explicitly interpretive table heading.

### Figure legends

- Identify the figure's subject, comparison, conditions, panels, and essential encodings.
- Define abbreviations, symbols, colors, line types, error bars, uncertainty intervals, and statistical annotations needed for independent interpretation.
- State the sample or unit of analysis when omission would make the display ambiguous.
- Describe what is displayed before offering any permitted interpretation.
- Do not claim a trend, difference, mechanism, or causal relationship that the plotted data and analysis do not establish.
- Keep panel labels, group names, units, and numerical terminology identical to the figure and manuscript.

## Cover Letters

- Address the correct editorial role. Do not invent an editor's name; use a role-based salutation when the name is not verified.
- Identify the manuscript title, article type, target journal, and corresponding author using supplied information.
- Summarize the research question, main contribution, and journal fit in specific, restrained language.
- Distinguish the study's contribution from unverified claims of novelty, priority, clinical impact, or policy impact.
- Include exclusivity, author approval, conflicts of interest, ethics, preprint, related-manuscript, or AI-use statements only when supported and appropriate to the venue.
- Do not duplicate the abstract. Explain why the work matters to the journal's readership.
- Keep the letter concise, professional, and free of pressure, flattery, or unsupported assertions.
- End with a direct request for consideration and accurate corresponding-author details.

Recommended order:

1. Salutation and submission identification.
2. Research problem, principal contribution, and restrained key finding.
3. Fit with the journal and likely relevance to its readers.
4. Required submission declarations supported by the supplied record.
5. Courteous closing and corresponding-author information.

## Reviewer Responses

- Treat the reviewer comment, the intended response position, the revised manuscript, and the change location as separate evidence sources.
- Preserve the author's intended stance: accept, partially accept, clarify, or respectfully disagree. Do not convert a qualified response into full agreement.
- Answer the actual concern before describing the edit.
- State precisely what changed and where. Do not claim that an analysis, experiment, citation, or textual revision was completed unless the revised manuscript confirms it.
- When disagreeing, acknowledge the concern, give a focused rationale supported by supplied evidence, and explain any clarifying manuscript change.
- Keep the tone professional and non-defensive. Remove gratitude formulas when repeated use makes the response mechanical, but retain an appropriate acknowledgment of the reviewer effort.
- Quote revised text only when the quoted wording matches the current manuscript exactly; otherwise summarize the change and give its location.
- Maintain a one-to-one mapping between comments and responses. Split compound comments into labeled subparts when necessary.
- Preserve uncertainty and limitations. Do not promise future work as though it has already been completed.
- Leave substantive response strategy, new analyses, and evidence decisions to the user or the designated research workflow.

Recommended response unit:

1. `Comment`: reproduce or accurately identify the reviewer concern.
2. `Response`: answer the concern and state the author's position.
3. `Change`: describe the verified manuscript revision or explain why no change was made.
4. `Location`: identify the current section, paragraph, table, figure, or line numbers when available.

## Final Genre Pass

Before returning the text:

- Confirm that the output follows the requested genre, language, structure, and length.
- Confirm that names, numbers, claims, citations, terminology, and manuscript locations match the supplied sources.
- Confirm that no required declaration or completed change was invented.
- Confirm that the prose remains appropriately restrained for the study design and publication stage.
- Complete the Final Pass in `editor-feedback-checklist.md`.
