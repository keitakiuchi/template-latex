## Scope and Authority

- Use these rules to improve argument structure, section function, reporting-aware prose, and claim calibration.
- Treat the supplied manuscript, analysis plan, protocol, preregistration, verified evidence map, tables, figures, and journal instructions as the authorities for study-specific content.
- Diagnose missing inputs when they block accurate writing. Do not create a research question, hypothesis, analysis, result, rationale, reporting declaration, or citation to make the prose appear complete.
- Keep substantive research design, evidence retrieval, citation verification, statistical reanalysis, and strategic peer review outside this skill. Route those tasks to the user or an appropriate research workflow.
- Follow verified target-journal instructions over this reference. When current journal policies or reporting-guideline versions matter, check official sources because requirements can change.

## Establish the Writing Contract

Before substantial drafting or revision, extract the following from the supplied materials:

1. Target genre, journal or audience, word limit, required structure, and English variety.
2. Primary research question and, if present, secondary or exploratory questions.
3. Research-question type and the corresponding ceiling on claims.
4. What prior work establishes and what remains unresolved.
5. The study's theoretical, methodological, or empirical contribution.
6. The scope beyond which the manuscript must not generalize.
7. The authoritative sources for methods, statistics, terminology, citations, and declarations.
8. The distinction among prespecified, secondary, sensitivity, and exploratory analyses.

Reduce the core argument to four internal one-sentence statements:

- `Question`: What problem does the study answer?
- `Gap`: What is known, and what remains inadequately explained or tested?
- `Contribution`: How does this study update understanding rather than merely add a result?
- `Boundary`: What can and cannot be concluded from the design and evidence?

If the supplied material does not support one of these statements, mark it as an unresolved source gap. Do not infer a stronger statement merely because it produces a smoother narrative.

## Calibrate Claims to the Research-Question Type

Use the study's stated objective and design, not the statistical technique alone, to identify the question type.

| Question type | Emphasize | Do not imply without additional support |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Descriptive | prevalence, distribution, frequency, characteristics, or observed patterns | causes, effects, mechanisms, prediction, or intervention benefit |
| Associational or explanatory | direction, magnitude, uncertainty, and plausible interpretation of relationships | causal effects when temporal order, identification assumptions, and design do not support them |
| Causal-effect estimation | the estimand, comparison, identifying assumptions, uncertainty, and sensitivity of the estimated effect | assumption-free causality, universal transportability, or mechanisms not evaluated |
| Prediction or classification | intended use, validation setting, discrimination, calibration, uncertainty, and generalizability | causation, explanatory importance, clinical or practical utility not evaluated in the decision context |
| Methodological or simulation | performance under defined scenarios, data-generating conditions, estimands, and evaluation metrics | universal superiority outside the simulated or evaluated conditions |
| Evidence synthesis | the eligible evidence, synthesis method, heterogeneity, bias, uncertainty, and limits of pooling | certainty or homogeneity unsupported by the included evidence |
| Qualitative or mixed methods | context, sampling logic, analytic process, patterns, interpretations, integration, and reflexive limits | population-wide frequency, causal effects, or generalizability beyond the analytic basis |

Treat an “explanatory” label conservatively when the supplied design supports association but not causal identification. Preserve modal verbs, caveats, assumptions, and alternative explanations across the title, abstract, main text, tables, and figures.

## Build the Manuscript Spine

Use the four writing-contract statements to create one continuous logic:

1. Establish an important phenomenon or problem.
2. Synthesize what existing evidence establishes.
3. Isolate a consequential gap, inconsistency, or boundary condition.
4. Explain how the present study addresses that gap.
5. Report methods capable of answering the stated question.
6. Present results in the order needed to answer that question.
7. Explain how the findings update understanding within the stated boundary.

Keep the central question singular or very limited in number. Identify one primary analytical line when the source materials do so, and label other analyses according to their documented status. Do not promote a secondary or exploratory result to the manuscript's main contribution merely because it is more striking.

Frame the contribution as an update to understanding. Specify what the findings clarify, extend, limit, distinguish, or make possible. Avoid treating statistical significance, a new dataset, a new population, or novelty alone as the contribution.

## Write for a Broad Scholarly Audience

- Preserve technical precision while reducing the effort required of readers outside the narrow specialty.
- Explain why the phenomenon or theoretical problem matters before introducing dense field-specific detail.
- Define indispensable specialist terms and abbreviations at the required first-use locations; remove those that do not earn their cognitive cost.
- Use informative topic sentences and explicit logical transitions when an inferential step would otherwise remain implicit.
- Connect a local empirical result to the broader theoretical question only within the study's claim boundary.
- Keep technical detail in the main text when readers need it to judge the claim. Move it to a supplement only when the journal permits this and the main text clearly signposts it.

## Assign One Function to Each Section

### Title and headings

- Make the topic, central relationship or phenomenon, context, population, and design discoverable when each is necessary for accurate interpretation.
- Prefer specific, searchable terms over promotional hooks.
- Keep the title inside the study's claim ceiling. Do not use causal, predictive, validation, effectiveness, or generalizability language that the manuscript does not support.
- Recast dense noun stacks and keep headings parallel at the same level.

### Abstract

- State the problem or gap, objective, methodological skeleton, principal concrete result, and bounded implication.
- Keep the question type and study design consistent with the title and main text.
- Report estimates and uncertainty when supplied and material; do not substitute significance alone for the principal result.
- Distinguish prespecified and exploratory findings when that distinction affects interpretation.
- Do not strengthen a conclusion merely to make the abstract more decisive.

### Introduction

Build the Introduction in four moves:

1. Explain why the phenomenon or problem matters to the journal's readership.
2. Synthesize the relevant state of knowledge.
3. Identify the consequential gap, inconsistency, or unresolved boundary.
4. State how the present study responds, what it contributes, and how far its claims extend.

Move the research question and contribution forward when background delays them. Avoid author-by-author inventories, gaps whose importance is unexplained, multiple competing objectives without a hierarchy, and promises that the later sections cannot fulfill.

### Theory or literature-review section

Make each paragraph perform one argumentative step:

1. State the paragraph's claim.
2. Integrate evidence relevant to that claim.
3. Explain convergence, disagreement, mechanism, limitation, or boundary conditions.
4. Bridge to the next proposition, hypothesis, research question, or implication.

Show why the proposed relationship or interpretation follows. Do not place multiple theories side by side without identifying their roles, predictions, points of tension, or the reason one is primary.

### Methods

- Write Methods so readers can judge both reproducibility and whether the method can support the manuscript's claim.
- Report study-specific details only from authoritative source material.
- Identify missing information explicitly rather than supplying plausible defaults.
- Explain the logic of a choice when the source gives a rationale; do not merely list method names.
- Preserve the distinction among the target population, source population, sample, unit of analysis, and final analytic set.
- Make definitions, measurement timing, data provenance, preprocessing, exclusions, missing-data handling, and analytic status traceable when relevant.
- Keep transparency statements factual: ethics, consent, registration, protocol, analysis plan, data and code availability, funding, conflicts, and AI use.

### Results

- Organize results in the order of the research questions, not the order in which analyses happened.
- Report participant or record flow, analytic-sample characteristics, primary results, secondary results, sensitivity analyses, and exploratory results in the documented hierarchy.
- Preserve exclusions, missingness, attrition, deviations, convergence failures, and null or uncertain results.
- Keep numbers, labels, directions, denominators, time points, and analysis status consistent across prose, tables, figures, abstract, and supplement.
- Report estimates, uncertainty, and relevant effect metrics when supplied. Reserve interpretation for the Discussion unless the target format requires combined sections.

### Discussion

Build the Discussion in the following sequence when compatible with the journal:

1. Answer the research question in calibrated language.
2. Interpret the main pattern of findings rather than repeating each result.
3. Explain how the findings clarify, extend, revise, or limit the theoretical account.
4. State a small number of evidence-bounded practical or methodological implications.
5. Explain limitations that materially constrain interpretation and identify proportionate next steps.

Avoid three common failures:

- `Rehashing`: repeating Results without interpreting their joint meaning.
- `Meandering`: listing disconnected implications or speculative explanations.
- `Overreaching`: moving beyond the question type, study design, assumptions, or observed evidence.

## Synthesize Literature into an Argument

Assign each citation a role: justify importance, define a construct, establish knowledge, expose a gap, position a contribution, justify a method, or calibrate interpretation.

- Begin paragraphs with the manuscript's claim, not an author's name, unless the author is itself the topic.
- Synthesize studies by agreement, disagreement, boundary condition, method, population, or unresolved issue.
- Prefer an appropriate review or meta-analysis for a broad consensus claim when a verified source is available; use primary studies for specific findings, mechanisms, methods, or exceptions.
- Think in claim-to-source units rather than requiring a citation after every sentence.
- Avoid one-study-per-sentence inventories and repeated citation clusters that obscure the paragraph's logic.
- Use direct quotation only when the wording itself is under analysis or is an authoritative definition that must be quoted.
- Draft only from verified or user-supplied references. Never invent a source, identifier, finding, or citation-to-claim relationship.
- Keep the author's interpretation visibly distinct from what cited studies establish.

## Check Reporting and Study-Type Signals

Use this section as a reporting-aware writing audit, not as permission to redesign the study or analysis.

### Observational and routinely collected data

Check whether the supplied materials support clear wording for the source and analytic populations, time zero, exposure, outcome, measurement timing, follow-up, censoring, confounding rationale, missingness, and sensitivity analyses. For routine data, also check database version or extraction date, coding algorithms, linkage quality, and the distinction between missing data and information never recorded.

### Simulation and methodological studies

Separate parameters derived from real data from assumptions chosen by the researchers. Check whether the source specifies the data-generating mechanism, scenarios, compared methods, performance measures, number of repetitions and rationale, Monte Carlo uncertainty, random-number handling, and convergence or numerical failures.

### Systematic reviews and meta-analyses

Check whether the source specifies eligibility, search and selection, extraction, risk-of-bias assessment, effect-size selection, dependence among estimates, synthesis model and rationale, heterogeneity, sensitivity analyses, small-study effects, certainty, and conditions under which pooling is inappropriate.

### Prediction and classification studies

Distinguish development from validation. Check whether the source supports wording about data splitting or resampling, leakage prevention, preprocessing, tuning, discrimination, calibration, internal versus external validation, intended use, and decision context.

### Qualitative and mixed-methods studies

Check whether the source supports wording about context, sampling, unit of coding, analytic steps, researcher or coder roles, negative or disconfirming cases, and the timing and method of integration in mixed-methods work.

### Reporting guidelines

Identify the applicable reporting guideline from the verified journal instructions, protocol, or study type early enough to shape the section outline and supplement. Examples may include guidance for observational studies, routinely collected data, prediction models, randomized trials, systematic reviews, qualitative research, or statistical reporting. Do not claim compliance from polished prose alone. Verify current names, versions, and journal requirements from official sources when they matter to the task.

## Preserve Transparency Without Inventing Declarations

- Distinguish prespecified, confirmatory, secondary, sensitivity, and exploratory work according to the supplied record.
- Do not describe a plan as preregistered, a protocol as public, data or code as available, or materials as reproducible without evidence.
- Preserve material deviations from a protocol or analysis plan and the timing of analytical decisions.
- Keep funding, conflicts, ethics, consent, data availability, code availability, authorship, and AI-use statements aligned with verified facts.
- Treat AI-disclosure requirements as venue-specific and changeable. If the user asks for a current disclosure, verify the journal's official policy and identify what the human authors checked and remain responsible for.

## Make the Manuscript Reviewer-Ready

Run a cross-section alignment audit:

| Element | Check across |
| --- | --- |
| Primary question and contribution | title, abstract, end of Introduction, Discussion opening |
| Study design and population | title when relevant, abstract, Methods, limitations |
| Primary outcome, exposure, intervention, or predictors | abstract, Methods, Results, tables, figures |
| Analysis status | Methods, Results, abstract, Discussion |
| Numerical results | abstract, Results, tables, figures, supplement |
| Claim strength and uncertainty | title, abstract conclusion, Results, Discussion, cover letter |
| Terminology and abbreviations | all sections, tables, figures, supplement |
| Transparency declarations | Methods, end matter, submission materials |

Check that each major method has a corresponding result, each Discussion claim traces to reported evidence, and each major limitation has a visible consequence for interpretation. Ensure that technical details moved to a supplement remain signposted in the main text.

For reviewer responses, distinguish a substantive change from a clarification of work already performed. State the verified change and location precisely. Do not imply that an analysis was prespecified or newly completed unless the record supports that statement.

## Final Argument and Reporting Pass

Before returning substantial drafted or revised prose, confirm that:

- The Question, Gap, Contribution, and Boundary can each be stated in one source-supported sentence.
- One central logical line connects the Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion.
- The title and abstract represent the actual question type and study design.
- The Introduction explains why the gap matters, not merely that a gap exists.
- Specialist terminology remains precise, but the argument is accessible beyond the narrow subfield.
- Literature-review paragraphs synthesize evidence and advance the manuscript's argument.
- Methods justify the claim's evidentiary basis without invented details.
- Results follow the research-question hierarchy and preserve uncertainty, null findings, and analysis status.
- The Discussion interprets rather than rehashes and stays within the claim ceiling.
- Confirmatory, secondary, sensitivity, and exploratory work are not conflated.
- Numbers, labels, terminology, citations, tables, figures, and supplement pointers remain aligned.
- Transparency and reporting statements are supported by the supplied record.
- Any missing source information is identified outside the proposed prose rather than silently fabricated.
- The Final Genre Pass and the checklist's Final Pass are complete.
